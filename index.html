Frequently Asked Questions

Everything You Need to Know

Clear answers about Kọmply, Nigerian compliance obligations and how it all works.

Do I need to file tax if my employer already deducts PAYE?
Yes — and this is the most common misconception in Nigeria. Even if your employer remits PAYE on your behalf, you are still legally required to file a personal annual tax return with your State Internal Revenue Service by March 31 every year. The 2026 NRS Act made this strictly enforceable with a ₦25,000 first-month penalty. Kọmply handles this entire process for you.
What is the penalty for late PAYE remittance?
Under the 2026 NRS Act, businesses that fail to remit PAYE by the 10th of each month face a ₦100,000 fine in the first month, then ₦50,000 for every subsequent month of default. Unpaid amounts also attract 10% interest per annum. Kọmply tracks your PAYE deadline and reminds you automatically before it is due.
How does Kọmply calculate PAYE?
Kọmply uses the official Nigerian PAYE tax bands: 7% on the first ₦300,000 of taxable income, 11% on the next ₦300,000, 15% on the next ₦500,000, 19% on the next ₦500,000, 21% on the next ₦1,600,000, and 24% on anything above ₦3,200,000. We also apply the Consolidated Relief Allowance (CRA) of ₦200,000 plus 20% of gross income before calculating tax.
What is the correct pension split in Nigeria?
Under the Pension Reform Act, both employer and employee contribute to a Pension Fund Administrator. The employee contributes a minimum of 8% of monthly emoluments and the employer contributes a minimum of 10% — totalling a minimum of 18%. Kọmply calculates both contributions automatically and tells you exactly when and where to remit each month.
I am a freelancer — do I need to pay tax in Nigeria?
Yes. All income earned in Nigeria — including freelance, consulting, rental and investment income — is taxable. Freelancers must register with their State Internal Revenue Service, file annual returns by March 31 and pay tax on net income. Kọmply's Personal plan is built specifically for freelancers and self-employed Nigerians.
